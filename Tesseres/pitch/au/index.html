03 / The policy gap

Promised, not delivered: the policy gap.

The federal government has acknowledged the risk - and then stepped back from the mechanisms that would manage it.

What was promised

  • 2024 - a Senate committee recommends mandatory safety rules for high-risk AI uses.
  • Enforceable obligations for the riskiest AI - not suggestions.
  • Dec 2025 - a National AI Plan with "big ambitions" for capability and safety.
  • A dedicated AI Safety Institute to monitor and manage risk.

What actually happened

  • Mandatory rules were shelved for a voluntary six-practice guidance note (Oct 2025).
  • The AI Safety Institute ($29.9M) can advise - it can't enforce anything.
  • Legal experts have publicly called the "existing laws are enough" position false hope.
  • No funded, at-scale program helps businesses adopt AI safely or retrain displaced workers.

Sources: Dept of Industry, Science and Resources (National AI Plan, Dec 2025; AI6 guidance, Oct 2025); White & Case; Montreal AI Ethics Institute; The Conversation, as at July 2026.

04 / The strategic position

Australia can't out-compute the superpowers - it can out-govern them.

The US and China own the AI engines, the chips and the computing power. Research on middle powers is blunt: countries that don't carve out a position lose control of their economy to it. Four realistic plays:

Specialise

Own one defensible piece of the global AI supply chain.

Align

Tie into one superpower's ecosystem and standards.

Share

Pool influence with other middle powers on AI rules.

Hedge

Blend capabilities from several sources; depend on none.

Tesseres' bet: specialise in the trust layer.

Not the AI engines or the chips - the safety, privacy and record-keeping layer that sits between foreign AI and Australian businesses. That's where trust, rules, data and jobs actually live - and it's the one part of the stack a country Australia's size can genuinely own.

Sources: Chatham House, "How middle powers can weather US and Chinese AI dominance", Feb 2026; IDC/Reuters chip-share data 2025; Bernstein 2026 forecast.

08 / Proof · TerranSoul

TerranSoul: AI memory, without the big-tech bill.

Small AI models on your own computers can act like the expensive ones - if you give them real memory instead of a bigger cloud bill.

Private by design

Runs on your own hardware. No documents or data ever leave the building to reach a foreign AI.

Costs almost nothing

Remembering and finding information costs close to $0 per query once it's running - no per-use cloud fees.

Safe by design

Anything suspicious is stopped before it enters long-term memory - the same discipline Tessera applies to code.

Measured, not claimed

Finds the right saved information 99.4% of the time on a 500-question standard memory test - in about a second, for $0 per query.

A frozen local model that scored 0 unaided reaches 10-20 in a long puzzle game with memory alone - no retraining.

In a public benchmark that rebuilds a Boeing 747 in 3D under a frozen local vision judge, human-owned knowledge taught into TerranSoul's memory raised the same frozen local model from 86.8 to 99.7 on a reference-parity index (reference build = 100) - no model change. A knowledge-transfer build on the same bench scores 108.9, above the reference build's own score under the identical judge - but it was seeded from a stronger AI's build (owner-authorised), then improved by the local model, so it is not pure self-improvement.

Founder-reported: learned 1 million résumés in ~20 minutes, then finds the top matches for a job ad - in any language - in under a minute (not independently audited).

Sources: TerranSoul Research, "Three Falsifiable Hypotheses About External Memory for Frozen Language Models" (2026) - see the paper for controls and limitations; TerranSoul Boeing 747 rebuild bench (Jul 2026) - frozen gemma4-12B vision judge, K=5 fixed-seed panel, v4 protocol; parity index = 100 × score / 63.92, the reference build's own score (raw: pure self-improvement 55.49, taught memory 63.71, knowledge transfer 69.63).
